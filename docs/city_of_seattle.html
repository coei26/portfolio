Introduction

This visualization was created from a dataset I transformed from the Seattle Police Department's Crime Data, which consists of offenses from the years 2008 to 2020. As a student going to school and living in the city of Seattle, I was particularly interested in conducting a more granular exploration of the different locations where assault crimes were most frequent over the years.

The Story

Where in Seattle do assault crimes most frequently occur? Whether it be a student deciding which routes to walk to get to class, new residents looking to buy a home, or even police departments deciding on specific patrol routes, knowing where assault crimes have most frequently occurred provide valuable information to any given person. Creating a visualization that answers this question can reveal important insights. For example, as a student at the University of Washington, zooming into the University District area will reveal that a greater number of assault offenses have occurred along University Way NE relative to other surrounding areas of the school. Therefore, when walking to class or alone at night, University Way NE may be an area to avoid. In this same way, zooming out of the map will show that the downtown areas of Seattle (Belltown, International District) have greater occurrences of assault crimes. In contrast, it is evident that some of the areas on the east side of Seattle such as Broadmoor, Denny Blaine and Madrona contain significantly less assault incidents. Therefore, these might be neighborhoods potential home buyers might consider, and interestingly, these happen to be some of the most affluent neighborhoods in the city of Seattle.

Dataset Analysis

The type of the transformed dataset is a multidimensional table. There are two keys, latitude and longitude, which are required to look up the value attribute n.

Column Data Type Semantics
latitude Position

This is spatial data that is paired with longitude to quantitatively describe a location on the Earth's surface. 		Key

This key attribute represents the angular distance of a place north or south of the Earth's equator in degrees. In this dataset, the latitudes are all within the city of Seattle.
longitude Position

This is spatial data that is paired with latitude to quantitatively describe a location on the Earth's surface. 		Key

This key attribute represents the angular distance of a place east or west of the meridian at Greenwich, England, expressed in degrees. In this dataset, the longitudes are all within the city of Seattle.
n Attribute

This is a specific property that is measured, observed, or logged. The type of this attribute is ordered quantitative data, as each number corresponds to a specific latitude-longitude pair. 		Value

This value attribute represents the total number of assault crimes recorded at each specific latitude-longitude pair from 2008 to 2020 in the city of Seattle. This number was a new attribute derived by filtering the "offense" column in the original data for any offenses relating to assault.

Design Choices

Design Choice Rationale
Using a point map Due to the nature of the question being about location, I chose to visualize the data using a map, given the latitudinal and longitudinal coordinates of the data. Since I was interested in the city of Seattle in particular, it was most effective to use points to denote locations rather than filled regions of space. Additionally, using points satisfied the granularity I was interested in exploring, as it is easy to locate where the greatest number of assault offenses occur down to a very specific location on a street.
Including a legend Including a legend makes it very clear to the viewer that each point in the map is scaled by size, and that each point represents the total number of assault offenses that have occurred at the location from 2008 to 2020. While the dot sizes might not seem most ideal, they do effectively communicate the range in number of assault offenses, with the maximum number of assault offenses at one location being 1,252.
Excluding (0,0) latitude-longitude values While filtering the data, I noticed that there were a number of assault offenses that had the value 0 for both latitude and longitude. Since the purpose of this visualization was to display different locations in Seattle where assault crimes occurred, including these points would not only be irrelevant to the question, but would also be inaccurate, as it is clear the location of these particular assault offenses were not available or documented.
Transforming original dataset I performed four main transformations to the original dataset.
  1. I grouped (aggregated) the data by the columns latitude and longitude.
  2. I filtered to exclude the latitude and longitude columns containing the value 0.
  3. I filtered the offense column to only include rows containing the word "Assault".
  4. I summed up the number of assault offenses for each latitude-longitude pair and created a new column, n to represent the numbers.
By transforming the original dataset, I was left with a smaller subset of data that consisted wholly of the relevant values used in my visualization. Additionally, by transforming the data, I was able to use a new column of data that allowed me to answer a more specific question and ultimately created a more interesting visualization.
Visually encoding by size To answer my question of locations where assault crimes most frequently occurred, I included the visual encoding of size. The greater the size, the greater the number of assault offenses that occurred at the location. As a result, encoding by size was effective in denoting locations where assault crimes more frequently occurred.
Using a grayscale color scheme While I could have encoded different colors for locations where the greatest numbers of assault offenses occurred, the size of the data made this unnecessary. Adding an encoding for color could also convolute the visualization, as size is already encoded. The black points nicely contrast the gray map background, making the map accessible to those with vision impairments, and using a grayscale makes this visualization accessible to those who may be colorblind.
